Sir Timothy John Berners-Lee

British Scientist

Sir Timothy John Berners-Lee (born 8 June 1955), also known as TimBL, is an English computer scientist best known as the inventor of the World Wide Web, HTML, the URL system, and HTTP. He is a professorial research fellow at the University of Oxford and a professor emeritus at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT).

Early Life & Education

  • Born: 8 June 1955, London, England, to mathematician parents involved with the first commercial computer, Ferranti Mark 1.
  • Schooling: Attended Sheen Mount Primary School, then Emanuel School (1969-1973).
  • University: Studied physics at The Queen's College, Oxford (1973-1976), graduating with a first-class BA. As a student, he built his own computer using discarded electronics.

Career Highlights

  • Early Career: Engineer at Plessey; helped create typesetting software at D. G. Nash.

Major Inventions and Contributions

  1. World Wide Web: Invented the World Wide Web in 1989 at CERN, combining hypertext with the Internet. His first proposal was presented in March 1989, and the first successful HTTP client-server communication occurred later that year. He implemented the first web browser/editor ("WorldWideWeb") and web server ("CERN HTTPd").
  2. HTML: Created the HyperText Markup Language, the code in which webpages are written.
  3. HTTP: Developed HyperText Transfer Protocol to move data on the web.
  4. URL: Designed the Universal Resource Locator system, now simply known as URLs.
  5. Advancing the Web: Established the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at MIT in 1994, ensuring open web standards.
  6. Solid & Inrupt: Leads projects like Solid and Inrupt for online privacy and data ownership.
  7. Advocacy: Vocal about net neutrality, open data, and affordable internet access.

Positions Held

Academic: Professorial research fellow at University of Oxford, professor emeritus at MIT, director/founder of W3C, co-founder of the Open Data Institute.

Other Roles: Advisory positions at foundations and technology organizations.

Awards & Honours

Turing Award (2016): For inventing the World Wide Web and fundamental web protocols.

Knighthood: Knighted by Queen Elizabeth II in 2004 for contributions to the global Internet.

Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering (2013) and numerous honorary degrees.
